1.1 Local Climate Change Projection

1.1 Collect data on local climate change projections


Local climate change projection provides information on changes in:


A. Seasonal temperature and seasonal rainfall in 2050 (2036-2065) and 2085 (2070-2099) based on the observed baseline data from 1971-2000. Refer to  Climate Information and Risk Analysis Matrix (CLIRAM) “Observed Climate Trends and Projected Climate Change in the Philippines" (PAGASA, 2018).


B. Number of extreme events including number of days with temperature >35°C, number of dry days with rainfall < 2.5 mm) number of days with extreme rainfall (> 150mm); and sea-level rise (if located along the coastline). Refer to Climate Change in the Philippines (PAGASA, 2011). Consult PAGASA for the most recent data on extreme event.


C. General changes expected in climate variables and information about patterns of change.


What is climate change? And what are Climate Change Scenario based on RCP?


Climate change is "any change in climate over time, whether due to natural variability or as a result of human activity” (IPCC, 2007). Climate projection is a “model-derived” estimates of future climate. Most recently, Representative Concentration Pathways (RCP) is the basis of climate change projection based on the trajectory of greenhouse gas concentration (IPCC, 2014). It was adopted by the IPCC for its fifth Assessment Report (AR5) in 2014 and has been used to project climate changes at international, national and subnational levels.  Four pathways (or RCPs) are proposed based on the level of concentration of greenhouse gas represented as watts per square meter (sqm).


    • RCP 2.6 -2.6 watts/ sqm
    • RCP 4.5 - 4.5 watts/ sqm
    • RCP 6 - 6.0 watts/ sqm
    • RCP 8.5 - 8.5 watts/ sqm


RCP 2.6 is the pathway where radical climate policy is adopted by nations to reduce the concentration of greenhouse gas. In contrary, 8.5 (RCP 8.5) is a climate change scenario in which carbon concentration that causes global warming is at average of 8.5 watts per sqm worldwide. This scenario also means an increased temperature of 4.3˚C by 2100 (when compared with baseline data). RCP 8.5 thus represents the worst-case scenario in terms of climate changes (i.e. increased temperature, extreme or lack of precipitation etc.) (Riahi et al, 2015).  


In the Philippines, based on RCP 8.5, PAGASA projects an increased of 1.2˚C to 2.3˚C by 2036-2065 and 2.5˚C to 4.1˚C by 2070-2099. Smaller values in terms of increased in temperature and rainfall changes are expected in lower RCPs (2.6, 4.5 and 6) (PAGASA, 2018). The recent publications by PAGASA (2018)  on climate change projections in the Philippines presents data on RCP 4.5 and RCP 8.5.


In this E-CDRA Training Module, RCP 8.5 is used as examples in tables and graphs emphasizing the advantage of planning for the worst-case scenario. But LGUs and other users can use other scenario (RCP 4.5) as basis of assessment and planning provided that they consult PAGASA and other government (e.g. CCC) and academic institutions with expertise on the matter.
