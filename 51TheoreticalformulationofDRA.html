5.1 Theoretical formulation of DRA


Data on climate change and hazards, and exposure units are gathered and used to facilitate Climate Risk Assessment (CRA) and Disaster Risk Assessment (DRA) (Step 4 and 5). CRA and DRA are mnemonic tools to assess vulnerabilities and risks in a given territory and period of time. Both tools measure vulnerabilities and risks based on the potential impacts of certain hazards to people and their properties. HLURB et al (2015: p. 11) proposes the use of tools in the following manner:


“Disaster Risk Assessment (DRA) uses historical patterns in describing climate-related hazards, [while] climate change vulnerability assessment or CCVA [referred to as Climate Risk Assessment or CRA in this manual] establishes how a changing climate may influence the frequency and severity of these hazards so actions for mitigation can be designed to accommodate predicted changes.”


DRA is used if there is an established historical pattern of hazard occurrence. Therefore, for vulnerability or risk assessment that deals with hazards that may happen in the future or are based on future climate scenarios, CRA is deemed more appropriate.  DRA is formulated as:

When to apply DRA


In theory, DRA may be conducted when the hazards and exposure units involve are:

    • Hydrometeorological and geological hazards with establish likelihood of occurrence.
      • Hydrometeorological hazards - rain-induced flooding, rain-induced landslide, storm surge, etc.
      • Geological hazards - groundshaking, liquefaction, tsunami, earthquake-induced landslide, etc.
      • Volcanic hazards - lahar, ashfall, pyroclastic flow, etc.
    • Exposure units involved are the five exposure units and other elements at risk identified in Step 3.


Use of probabilistic hazard maps


Probabilistic hazard mapping considers different scenarios for any given hazard based on its known characteristics (i.e. magnitude, intensities, etc.). These characteristics of hazards may be certain (if based on previous experience) or uncertain (if based on simulation of future scenarios i.e. climate change), or a combination of both. Probabilistic hazard maps can then reflect the increasingly unpredictable nature of hazards particularly hydrometeorological hazards (i.e. flood, typhoon, landslide, etc.) brought by climate change. And through simulation of different hazard scenarios, several hazard maps for a single hazard can be produced and used for risk assessment i.e. 5, 25, and 100-year return period flood map.
